There is a material this season that smells like sunshine before you even touch it. Lift a raffia tote off a market table in the south of France, press your cheek against the brim of a wide sun-hat in a shop on the Amalfi coast, and the scent drifts up instantly — warm hay, toasted grass, a little honey, a little salt. It is the smell of summer distilled into a fibre, and for Spring 2026, raffia has taken over the boho imagination like a long, lazy afternoon you never want to end.

Raffia is harvested from the fronds of the *Raphia* palm, a tree that grows tall and graceful along the rivers and coastlines of Madagascar, where it has been woven into baskets, mats, and ceremonial cloth for centuries. In the highland markets of Antananarivo, you can still watch the whole slow ritual unfold: long green fronds are split into whisper-thin ribbons, laid out to dry in the equatorial sun until they turn the colour of pale honey, then twisted between a weaver's palms into a strand as strong as linen and as soft as a sigh. It is one of those crafts that refuses to be rushed. A simple market tote takes a full day of patient coil-work; a wide-brimmed sun-hat, several. And you can feel that slowness when you wear it — a raffia accessory has a kind of quiet presence, a rustle that belongs to another, gentler clock.

What makes Spring 2026's raffia moment feel fresh is the way designers and boho stylists are layering it into looks that aren't trying to look "beachy" at all. The trick is contrast. Pair a coarse, hand-coiled raffia tote with something featherlight and feminine on top — a soft white cotton, a bit of ribbon, a flutter of embroidery — and suddenly the texture sings.
