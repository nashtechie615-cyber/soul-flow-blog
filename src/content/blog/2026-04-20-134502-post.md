There is a particular kind of skirt that does not merely fall — it *unfolds*. Tier by tier, ruffle by ruffle, a gentle architecture of cotton and gauze that begins at the waist as a whisper and ends at the ankle as a small, exuberant declaration. This spring, after seasons of slip skirts and clean A-lines, the tiered skirt has returned to the front pages of every style journal we love, and Soul Flow Apparel is meeting the moment with the kind of soft, sun-warmed pieces that make the silhouette feel less like a trend and more like a homecoming.

Call it the gypsy skirt, the prairie skirt, the broomstick skirt, the crinkled gauze tier — the names change by decade, but the spirit is unmistakable. A tiered skirt is a skirt with chapters. Each gathered seam is a small horizon line, a place where the fabric pauses, breathes, and begins again. Walk in one and you will feel it before you see it: the upper tier sways slowly against your hip, the middle tier follows half a beat later, and the bottom tier — the longest, the loosest, the most free — finishes the sentence with a flutter that brushes the tops of your feet. It is movement in three voices, and once you have worn one for a full day, the straight-line pencil skirts of the world begin to feel a little flat.

What makes spring 2026's iteration so particular is its softness. The runways and street style feeds out of Copenhagen, Jaipur, and the Hudson Valley have all converged on a tiered skirt that is unapologetically *romantic* — pulled in slow gauze cottons, sun-faded florals, eyelet-pierced poplins, and the occasional whisper of crinkled silk. Hems graze the ankle rather than the calf. Waistbands are smocked, drawstrung, or elasticated rather than zipped. The whole garment is built to be lived in — to be packed in a tote bag, pulled on damp from the sea, layered over a swimsuit, slept in, danced in, eventually loved into the kind of softness that only really earned clothes ever achieve.
