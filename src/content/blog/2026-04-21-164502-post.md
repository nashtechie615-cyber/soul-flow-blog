There is a certain kind of trim that refuses to take itself seriously, and in the spring of 2026, it is having a very loud, very joyful moment. I'm talking about the pom-pom — that little round tuft of yarn, no bigger than a robin's egg, sewn along a hem or dangling from a drawstring like a tiny cheerleader for your outfit. Pom-poms are what happens when a textile decides to giggle. They are the clothing equivalent of walking into a room with fresh flowers in your hair.

This season, they are everywhere. Down the runways at resort shows, along the edges of breezy market totes, bouncing at the ankles of wide-leg linen pants, circling the brims of straw sun hats like a parade of marshmallow planets. And the romance behind them runs deeper than you'd think. Pom-poms have lived for centuries in the textile traditions of [Mexico, Guatemala, Peru, and the Andean highlands](https://soulflowshop.com) — where women wound bright wool around their fingers, tied the middles, snipped the loops, and trimmed the little spheres into perfect fluffy orbs. They were stitched onto shawls, blankets, ceremonial hats, and the corners of babies' swaddles as talismans of protection and celebration. To wear them is to carry a little piece of that joy with you.

What I love about pom-poms is how forgiving they are. They soften every silhouette they touch. A plain white cotton tunic becomes instantly charming the moment a row of cream pom-poms marches along its hem. A drawstring waistband on a pair of linen pants becomes a secret delight when the ties end in two little puffballs that swing when you walk. Even a simple tote becomes a conversation piece when a trio of multicolored pom-poms dangles from its strap like fruit on a vine.

The trick to wearing pom-pom trim without veering into costume territory is restraint. Let the pom-poms be the only extra voice in your outfit. Tonal trim — ivory pom-poms on ivory cotton, sand on sand, pale pink on pale pink — reads as sophisticated and sculptural rather than souvenir-shop.
